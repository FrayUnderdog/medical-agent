# Fever (raised temperature)

## Overview
A fever means your body temperature is higher than usual. Infections are a common cause, but other conditions can contribute. This page offers general self-care ideas only and does not replace a clinician’s assessment.

## What you can do now
- Rest and drink water or oral rehydration fluids if tolerated.
- Light clothing and a comfortable room temperature may help.
- Track how you feel over time; note the highest temperature if you measure it.

## Red flags
- Confusion, severe drowsiness, or difficulty waking.
- Trouble breathing, blue lips, chest pain, or fainting.
- A stiff neck with fever, a rapidly spreading rash, or signs of dehydration (very dry mouth, barely passing urine).
- If you are pregnant, immunocompromised, very young, or recently returned from travel with high fever — seek urgent advice.

## When to seek medical advice
- Fever lasting more than three days, or getting worse instead of better.
- Severe pain, repeated vomiting, or you feel substantially worse than with a typical cold.
